Welcome to the Cycle of Practice

This site summarizes the insights of an observational learning program, conducted by our team of design researchers with the goal of facilitating the beginning of the co-design process by providing teams with contextual understanding of teachers, their practices, classrooms, and students. The framework and teacher modes on this site are the result of 63 one-on-one teacher interviews, 41 classroom observations, and 20 virtual classroom deep dives, conducted with 18 teachers (8 from one district, 7 from a second district, and 3 from a third district) over the 2019-20 school year. We hope that this site helps co-design teams to think about multiple perspectives and engage in further conversation about the classrooms of teachers.

Cycle of Practice

This summary is organized in a framework called the Cycle of Practice. The Cycle describes one model of the beliefs, knowledge, and actions of teachers in a six stage loop that follows how teacher mindsets and identities come to life in classroom behaviors. This framework is meant to provide co-design teams with insights and discussion prompts to guide their design and development efforts. We hope that co-design teams can use the Cycle framework to consider how prototype learning systems resonate with teacher mindsets and how features can best support teacher practices. For example, a team might brainstorm on a specific stage within the Cycle, digging deep into this aspect of teacher practice to imagine ways that their solution can meet the needs of a range or teachers and reflect the values that they have.

Teacher Modes

We also found four teacher modes in our learnings from our district partners. Each teacher mode embodies a unique approach to the Cycle of Practice. Each teacher mode reveals a different set of teacher beliefs and reveals how these beliefs come to life in classrooms. Each mode is a composite of the teachers collaborated with throughout this learning project, a flexible set of descriptors that are meant to inspire new thinking (not put teachers into categories). We hope that these teacher modes can spark new ideas in the co-design teams and act as filters as teams iterate solutions. For example, teams can ideate functions, features or design attributes for each teacher mode. An exercise like this will help a team to push the edges of their solution, exploring potential barriers to adoption and imagining how the solution will be considered and used by different teachers in different modes. We have included a downloadable resource with some prompting questions at the end of each teacher mode that we hope can support teams in this work.

Executive Functions

It’s worth noting that, though EFs are central to this program, the teachers we interviewed did not discuss EFs by name without prompting. When discussing EFs through our prompts, differences were noted in how teachers discussed EFs and the ways researchers discuss EFs. This, of course, is the opportunity that this program is addressing.

Generally, when asked about EFs, teachers described student emotions, behaviors, and social emotional learning. Teachers discussed working memory in connection to student prior knowledge, recall, muscle memory, and tools such as student notebooks and notes. Teachers discussed cognitive flexibility as related to students’ use of different math strategies or approaches, student ownership of learning, and student problem solving. Inhibitory control was described by teachers through the lens of student behavior, independence, perseverance, self-efficacy, and relationships with peers.

Because our goal in this site is to bring forward teacher mindsets and contexts, we use this teacher-driven language throughout.

Equity

Similarly, throughout the site we strive to frame teachers’ equity approaches and practices in their own language and mindsets.

Teachers discussed how inequities impact the contexts of their classroom and their students’ lives. Teachers were most likely to talk about challenges related to poverty and said that these could be barriers for learning, for example, if a student was coming in hungry or preoccupied with family stress. They also spoke about how their English language learning students were impacted in their ability to access content; for example, struggles with word problems were a common theme.

Some teachers explicitly enacted tools and structures to support equity in their classrooms. The most commonly seen were methods to ensure that calling on students to speak was randomized and evenly distributed. Teachers’ approaches to small group and individual work also emphasized students who most needed the additional support based on their current performance.

We hope this document serves as a starting point to further discussions among co-design teams about how equity can inform classroom practices.

COVID-19 Response

Finally, this program and the classrooms studied were disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic. There are additional insights revealing how the Cycle of Practice was impacted by remote learning. We interviewed teachers at the beginning of Summer 2020 about their experiences from the mid-March closure through the end of the school year. These interviews all took place before teachers were preparing for the 2020-21 school year.

Research Methods

×

This observational learning program was conducted by our team of design researchers to provide insight into the contexts of math classrooms and the mindsets and behaviors of teachers.

Design research is a generative approach to learning about what the world looks like through the eyes of users. It’s focused on setting aside preconceptions so that we can listen closely, look anew, and gain empathy. Design research surfaces insights about what people value, aspire to, and perceive, as well as what they experience, are challenged by, and need. Its goal is to identify actionable insights and opportunities for design.

Participants

Through this program, we came to know 18 teachers in districts around the country. Three districts were recruited by the EF+Math Program as both partners in the co-design program and as participants in this initial learning.

Teachers were selected by their districts for participation in the co-design program and learning. These teachers reflected a range of backgrounds, tenures, and grade levels.

Learning interactions

The program was disrupted, as so many things, by the COVID-19 pandemic, so methods shifted midway. Teachers were asked to participate in individual interviews and either classroom observations or lesson deep dives.

For the teachers that we visited in person, we conducted our interviews in person, in their classrooms, perched in kid-sized chairs. We observed their classes directly, as well as their team meetings, and interviewed the math leaders in their schools.

For the teachers we met after the pandemic set in, we met virtually. Interviews were conducted by video conference and in lieu of classroom observations, we conducted lesson deep dives, in which teachers replayed a lesson for us.

Teacher interviews

Interviews were shaped around the following topics (across either two (pandemic) or three (pre-pandemic) interviews). The goal of the interviews was to understand teachers’ perspectives, experiences, and contexts to provide opportunities for the R&D teams to co-design solutions that reflect these values, needs, and realities.

Our team designed our interview topics around a model developed to apply research on behavior change to design opportunities. This model was developed based on a review of literature around adherence and behavior change in the psychology, medicine, and nursing practices, including influential work such as the research of James Prochaska and Robin DiMatteo. These findings from academic research were combined with best practices from designers working in the behavior change space to create a set of tools that are both theoretically rigorous and also useful, usable, and desirable for the practice of designing interventions.

Observations / lesson deep dives

To ground the observations in sound math instruction, our team was normed on key practices (a crosswalk of NCTM Principles to Action (2014) and Teaching for Robust Understanding (Schoenfeld, 2014)). Our goal was not to evaluate teachers, but to center our observations on barriers and enablers of critical practices.

Classroom observations were shaped around a common design thinking framework, in which the design researchers observed for the following areas. This framework allows design researchers to focus attention on critical elements of the context, with the goal of identifying design opportunities.

  • Activities: what are people doing?
  • Environment: how do people navigate within the classroom?
  • Interactions: what are people doing together?
  • Objects: what is being used by people

Lesson deep dives in the virtual setting were not able to replicate the experience of being in classrooms as learning was happening, but we sought to investigate the areas of highest relevance, based on analysis of the direct observations. We asked teachers to pick one pre-pandemic lesson for each deep dive and to walk us through the lesson as it rolled out.

  • Activities: teachers discussed the activities planned in the lesson, how they were implemented, and how they used different structures throughout the lesson
  • Environment: teachers drew us a plan of their classroom and talked through why it is designed as it is
  • Interactions: teachers reflected on questions student had during the lesson as well as the questions they asked of students
  • Objects: teachers discussed what tools were used during the lesson

Analysis

Our team worked in pairs to interview and observe teachers and coded this data (interview notes, video and audio recordings) as teams. Designers coded the data for areas of interest independently and then met to align on any areas of disagreement (there were few). Group analysis surfaced themes within and across these initial areas of interest, which became the key factors highlighted in the final site.

We returned to the model of adherence and behavior change to structure our analysis, aligning key factors to the stages of this model to create the Cycle of Practice framework.

To bring this framework to life and to contextualize how it unfurls for different types of teachers, we then analyzed participants to identify clusters of beliefs and practices. Key factors were articulated for each of the teachers who we learned from. For factors drawn from teachers’ self-reporting, the team ranked teachers high/medium/low against a common rubric so that trends could be identified. For factors related to observable behaviors, these were coded for each participant. The team worked together to look across factors, across participants, identifying teacher modes that interacted with the framework in distinct ways.

COVID-19 Remote Learning

Teachers were interviewed again in early summer 2020 about their experiences with remote learning in the spring. The themes here don’t reflect planning or approaches to the 2020-21 school year.

The abrupt switch to remote learning was universally problematic for teachers and their students. It challenged teachers’ conceptions of their own work and effectiveness and disrupted the roles that they value for themselves. The transition was traumatic and uncertain, sticking clearly in peoples’ minds like those blue skies on September 11th. The move to remote learning was messy, disheartening, and disruptive to each phase in their Cycle of Practice.

I look back now and think how much we didn’t know.

Virtual school is not my cup of tea.

Nobody knew how long it would be or how difficult.

BELIEVE

The sudden switch to remote learning fundamentally called into question teachers’ beliefs about their work and their students’ learning.

The shift to remote dramatically lowered teachers’ expectations.

The measure of their work became about engagement and participation rather than academics, with the goal of staying connected with students and families. Teachers said that any work was good work, delighted to receive proof of effort even when the caliber was far below what they would expect under normal circumstances.

And even this was challenging. Student participation felt low and sporadic. Teachers anecdotally described their students falling into the following types of engagement.

  • Consistent participation: most teachers said there were a handful of students who they could count on to show up for meetings and/or to complete all the assigned work. This was on the order of 20-30% of their class (these numbers are estimates).
  • Sporadic participation: the bulk of the class would participate sporadically, jumping in some days or weeks and then falling off the radar. This was on the order of half the class based on teacher recollections.
  • No participation: finally, there were students who didn’t participate at all and who teachers may not have been able to reach at all during remote learning. This was on the order of 20-30%, according to teacher estimates.

Participation itself was varied, from students who’d show up, do the work and reflect effort, to students who would only log on to meetings, to students who’d attempt work but with low apparent effort (though teachers noted that family/home circumstances made it difficult to judge true interest/effort/accessibility).

I really had to put higher academic expectations on the back burner because I was trying to get involvement.

Compromised teaching

Given how hard it was for teachers to encourage students to show up and demonstrate engagement, teachers believed there was little substantive teaching and learning happening in their classes. Teachers said the process felt like treading water, that they had little ability to reach and influence students in positive ways.

There was no teaching, I didn’t teach anything.

I felt like a Kahn Academy teacher. It’s not effective for my students.

Teacher self-blame

A number of teachers described how hard remote learning was on them, talking about their feelings of inadequacy and ineffectiveness. Remote learning during shut-down made it near impossible for teachers to execute on their beliefs about teaching and learning. For some, this sparked considerable self-blame.

It definitely felt like I wasn't doing enough. I’m missing something. I’m not doing as much as I can, that insignificant feeling of I'm not doing as much as I could. It was tough to work through.

It really did take a toll on me too. What did I do wrong?

FRAME

Remote learning recast teachers’ mental models of their roles and effectiveness. Their normal conceptions of their identities were challenged by the pressures of remote learning and the pandemic.

Teachers as a communication hub

Much of teachers’ jobs became about communication with parents and students. Initially, teachers described helping families manage the confusion over the transition to remote learning, then the bumps of acclimating to technology. Mostly, though, teachers recounted a long slog of reminders, pinging students and parents to complete classwork work and attend synchronous sessions.

I just tried to be as encouraging as I could. I didn’t want to bring any negativity into the situation. It put a lot of stress on me. I’m trying to help all these people, but me myself I’m going through all this as well.

Emphasizing wellbeing over academics

This increased communication with families went well beyond academics. Teachers absorbed the burden of worrying about (and supporting) the health and wellness of their students and, at times, their families. For some teachers, this meant dropping off groceries or being a friendly ear for a family’s stresses. For others, it was about supporting students with the mental health impacts of school closure.

I shifted to the idea that this academic part is not going to matter in the scheme of things. They’re going to come out of it with gaps and with trauma. I’d rather focus on healing the child than their [COVID-exacerbated] academic gaps. This is out of our hands.

New contexts for learning

In normal times, teachers talk about the challenges in students’ lives that impact their work in the classroom. During remote learning, the classroom was coming into students’ home contexts. This posed profound challenges for teachers and students.

Remote learning required a degree of independence from students that teachers did not see as reasonable for some students. It asked students to balance learning with home responsibilities, such as caring for siblings. And many teachers said that learning was more reliant on parents’ ability to support students at home.

Kids that worked for me in class, they weren’t performing. It was very confusing for me. They have the ability and they weren’t doing it at home. It dawned on me when I saw them in Zoom meetings and they’re laying down, obviously there are things happening at home.

Heroic acts of teaching

These new contexts and roles meant that teachers weren’t able to teach as they normally do. The roles and moves that are typically their foundations were thrown into question and the activities supported by remote learning were faint echoes of their usual teacher selves. Some teachers told stories of Hail Mary activities or bright spot projects they designed -- heroic efforts that, at least, made them feel like they’d given it their all.

When I was prime stressing out, I printed out two books at each kid’s level. I’d tried lots of channels, so let’s try mail, so I know that I couldn't do anything differently. It said three stamps, but I put four on because I was stressed out.

KNOW

As teachers describe remote learning, there was no blueprint, no model of success. There still isn’t. They were left with few supports and a great deal of ambiguity about what “good” meant in this context.

Change was constant

Not surprisingly, district guidance was inconsistent. Transitions were tough. School closures were announced without clear guidance for teachers available. As spring break 2020 came and went, districts got their feet beneath them and were able to offer more coherent direction to teachers.

Even the best district guidance, however, was not enough to level up teachers and equip them for this challenge within the tight timeframe of the Spring shutdown. Teachers expressed that directives generally focused on staying engaged and ensuring equitable experiences rather than rigorous academics and that district models, when they existed, required customization for their classes.

Everybody was in stasis. Something new came down every day. It really felt like doing nothing, we were still contacting kids but there was no curriculum.

There wasn’t a lot of [district] guidance in what we were supposed to do. We don’t know what to do or how to do it, but good luck!

Lack of accountability

Teachers believe that a key challenge in keeping students and their families invested in education was the fact that districts announced early that families and students would not be held responsible for schoolwork during remote learning. Districts announced that students wouldn’t be held accountable for remote learning, and teachers reflected a narrow range of approaches to what this looked like, from an end of year pass/fail (in which everyone passed), to grading only up to March, to no grades but short descriptions of the student’s work.

The district didn’t want us to give them any work. They said this is more serious than school.

Planning was totally new

Remote learning disrupted teacher routines, often resulting in an ambient always-working mode. Teachers were able to plan and structure their own work, creating assignments, recording videos, and posting them at routine times. But they weren’t able to anticipate when students and families would need their attention, with students turning things in from morning to night and many teachers feeling like their quick feedback was a way to show care and connection.

Some teachers were thrown into isolation by remote learning, while others continued to plan as a team. Teamwork could be helpful, when it allowed teachers to play to their strengths and divvy up work; or, it could add burden, when one teacher had more tech capabilities or fewer other at-home responsibilities than their teammates.

I worked longer hours, getting assignments ready for the kids. I’d start at 6:30[am], make sure assignments are put in Google Classroom, wake up in the morning and do the video overview of what to do for the day. Then copy that and put in Class Dojo for parents…

Here it was just, ‘Oh my god, I have to make this video, oh and give feedback.’ It takes so much more time than just looking over a shoulder.

ACT

Everything changed about the contexts teachers were building and responding to during remote learning.

Challenges with technology access

Technology and connectivity access was a barrier throughout. Even as districts scrambled to get tech into each family’s hands, access was not equal. Some families shared a computer across multiple family members, some had students working from tablets or phones, some families (and teachers) had spotty to no wifi.

Even with tech in hand, there was a considerable learning curve to get students and families comfortable on the range of platforms, as teachers simultaneously experimented with what would work best for them and their students. Teachers were tech-support in many cases, making how-to videos or hopping on calls to walk frustrated families through the process.

In this uneven, inequitable landscape, guidance was to set a baseline that was not tech-dependent so that there could be equitable access. For some teachers, this meant they were getting direction not to offer any synchronous learning that relied on technology. For some, it meant they needed to offer options, so that students could participate through a range of channels.

Loss of connections with students

Teachers keenly felt the loss of connection with their students and their classroom community. Remote learning damaged the culture of the classroom, as teachers described students as being on islands on their own. For both teachers and students, the ability to connect directly was lost, mimicked in comment boards or social Zoom calls. Teachers described the missing element as the in-the-moment ability to connect, to be able to gauge how a student is, to give a hug or a high five, or to peek over a shoulder to see how the work is going.

It’s twice removed, you’re not even near me and you have a short attention span and I can't reassure you as much as I can in a small group setting.

I definitely think it’s more difficult for them. They’re kind of out on an island.

It’s a lot different. I don’t get to physically give somebody a pat on the back or put my hand on a shoulder and redirect them. There are no high fives, no knuckles in class.

Loss of control

In a multitude of ways, teachers felt their loss of control in remote learning. From the moment schools transitioned to remote, teachers experienced this loss. Many schools directed teachers to hold information until the last minute so as not to add to students’ concerns and confusion and to ensure the most up-to-the-moment facts were presented; teachers reflected regrets that they didn’t get to better prepare students for remote learning tools, expectations, and routines.

Students working independently in their own contexts meant that teachers lost the ability to manage how they structured their time and work, and the mindsets teachers attempt to set in the classroom to motivate students to learn. Teachers also expressed how challenging it was not to be able to see what their students were doing, losing the ability to course-correct in real time or to enable students to build off each others’ thinking.

Perhaps most acute was the loss of the ability to teach the way they love to. Remote learning made some teachers say they had reverted to direct instruction and procedural practice, losing the ability to foster student conversation, collaboration, and responsiveness to a range of ideas and methods.

I had to release a lot of control and be at peace with how terrible the situation is and how cruddy the quality was, the hack job.

I much prefer the classroom in terms of academics because I can hold them accountable. I can’t do this! I have no control over ‘you’re going to sit there and you have to do this.’ I hated it. I hated that part of remote learning.

Developing new routines

Despite all of these challenges, teachers responded with routines and structures for their students. The main variations included:

  1. Daily: some teachers attempted to keep routines not dissimilar from in-person learning, with morning meetings and daily assignments
  2. Weekly: some teachers’ schools or teams broke content up over the week, with certain days flagged as math days
  3. Menus: others posted menus, allowing students to select weekly which activities they chose to complete

Across these structures, teachers relied on the following channels and tools:

  1. Synchronous calls (group, small group, 1:1), including morning meeting style sessions that kicked off the day, math lessons, or office hours for students with questions, and social/wellbeing check-ins intended solely as means of connecting
  2. Videos, either off-the-shelf or teacher-made, focused on math instruction and sometimes walking students through expectations for the day/week
  3. Assignments such as Google Slides/Forms, worksheets
  4. Work on platforms, like iReady, MobyMax, or Prodigy

You try to build that culture again but you don’t have the info to do it.

Usually I’m thinking about what kinds of methods I want to see them using or ideas I want to build off of so I can quickly look at this kid is zoning in on this. There’s none of that [in remote learning]; it’s all me and my ideas and here’s how i’m telling you to approach the problem.

ADAPT

In some ways, remote learning put teachers into hyper-adaptive mode, as they hustled to get wholly new ways of teaching and learning up and running. In other ways, it hampered their ability to respond and react to their students.

Adaptations to formats and tools

Throughout remote learning, teachers were iterating on platforms, tools and assignments to figure out what worked best for them and their students. They were adjusting district recommendations to their own students and classrooms and trying a range of formats to see what students would respond to and what would work across technology and analog modes. Teachers would see where they got more or less engagement and where students and families were challenged by the technology as input for adaptation.

It went from me churning out material, to realizing that kids were not engaging at the level I wanted, to being told my principal wants it this very specific way to be in alignment with legal stuff and standards.

It changed every single day based on what we learned.

I stopped using interactive Google Slides because I noticed a lot of kids weren’t doing them on computer, but on iPad or phone, so having to do Google Slides was difficult. I moved assignments from something that had to be done online to see it on device and do work on paper, like task cards on Google Slides that you can type online or can write on paper, then snap a picture and have mom/dad/grandma/grandpa send it on Dojo.

Teacher blind spots

The distance between students and teachers hamstrung teachers in their ability to be most responsive. Teachers described attempting to be quick and encouraging in their feedback to student work, in the hope that it would motivate students and demonstrate their care.

But feedback felt like a one-way street. Student work wasn’t coming in regularly or at the quality level of in-person learning, so it was difficult to understand where students were at. And teachers weren’t sure their feedback was read by students. Even if it was, it was a far cry from the in-the-moment corrections that they are able to give in the classroom.

There was a lot more struggling. In the classroom I’m looking to fix things straight away, but here you’re correcting and hoping they are looking back.

Teacher effort

Teachers said that this lack of return resulted in them metering their own effort. Multiple teachers said that early on they dove in head first, creating differentiated assignments or filming multiple videos of themselves each day. As time passed and they saw only a handful of students engage, it made them question their own effort and scale it back.

I started doing videos of myself going through math pages. The videos were good! I felt like they were ok. But the amount of time it was taking and the amount of kids doing the work, it didn’t seem like a good use of my time.

As a teacher, we teach, that’s our job. But it’s hard to keep giving out and not getting that return. You’re doing all that work and you only have 5-8 kids coming and those are the ones who don’t need your help.

REINFORCE

With such isolation from their students and colleagues, teachers struggled to find meaningful feedback on their work.

You can’t put your finger on it because you’re not there.

Lack of visibility into student work

Most essentially, teachers were stripped of the flow of student work that they are accustomed to. Challenged by the struggles of remote learning, students were accomplishing much less work, not turning anything in, or doing so only occasionally. As a result, teachers were given only a patchy view of their students’ understanding and progress.

Moreover, the work that students were turning in was at a lower caliber than teachers were used to. Students weren’t necessarily aware of their gaps and teachers had a tough time making sure that feedback or corrections were landing with them to course correct misconceptions.

The mechanics of work could be a barrier as well. Blurry phone pics of a worksheet were difficult to read and impossible to offer meaningful feedback on.

My ability to give feedback was totally destroyed. In the normal classroom, I feel like I have a real solid idea of the handful of kids I need to check in with tomorrow or the kids having the interesting conversation to dive deeper into tomorrow.

The return is students doing the work. It was hard not to see assignments being turned in. And the quality of the work [was low]! What’s going on, why can’t they do it on their own? It makes you reflect back on you.

Bright spots

Some teachers found or made their own bright spots. Online platforms offered unique opportunities for differentiation without students being sensitized to it. For example, teachers could assign work individually without students comparing with peers.

And moments that reinforced relationships and the classroom community shone, such as end-of-year share outs that reflected classroom values, birthday cards between students, or even moments of affection in a Zoom chat.
